Writing an Opinion Essay?

How to Sound 'Objective'

How can an opinion be objective? This is a common question. The answer is simple:

  • Your STANCE is subjective (it's your personal opinion).
  • Your TONE must be objective (it must sound formal, calm, and logical).

Think of it this way: You are a lawyer in a courtroom. You have a strong opinion (your client is innocent), but you must persuade the judge with logic and evidence, not by shouting or crying.

Rule #1: Don't Exaggerate or Over-generalize

Words like all, never, everyone, or always are "absolute" words. They are usually untrue and make your argument sound weak and childish.

What to AVOID (Weak) What to DO (Strong)
"Everyone knows that mobile phones are bad for children." "It is often argued that mobile phones can have negative effects on children."

More Examples:

  • Avoid: "People never read books anymore because of the internet."
    Do: "The internet may have reduced the time many people spend reading traditional books."
  • Avoid: "All politicians are corrupt."
    Do: "Corruption can be a significant problem within some governments."
  • Avoid: "You always get a good job if you go to university."
    Do: "A university education tends to improve a person's employment prospects."

Rule #2: Don't Be Too Emotional

Your argument is stronger when it is based on logic, not feelings. Avoid "emotional" words.

What to AVOID (Weak) What to DO (Strong)
"I hate the idea of junk food. It's disgusting!" "Junk food is often criticized for its poor nutritional value and links to health issues."

More Examples:

  • Avoid: "It's crazy to let teenagers have social media!"
    Do: "The impact of social media on teenagers is a serious concern for many parents and experts."
  • Avoid: "The new city park is wonderful and the best thing ever!"
    Do: "The new city park provides significant social and environmental benefits to the community."
  • Avoid: "I feel angry when I see so much plastic waste."
    Do: "Plastic waste is a pressing environmental problem that requires urgent action."

Rule #3: Avoid Making All Examples About You

Your personal life is not strong evidence. Make your examples broader and more academic.

What to AVOID (Weak) What to DO (Strong)
"My friend tried to learn Spanish online and he failed. This shows online learning is bad." "For some students, online learning can be difficult if they require face-to-face interaction."

More Examples:

  • Avoid: "In my high school, we had to wear uniforms, and we all hated it."
    Do: "School uniforms are often unpopular with students, who may feel they limit personal expression."
  • Avoid: "My dad works 10 hours a day and is always tired, so long hours are bad."
    Do: "In many modern workplaces, long working hours are a common cause of employee stress and burnout."
  • Avoid: "I use social media every day, and it doesn't make me sad."
    Do: "The psychological impact of social media can vary from person to person."

Your 'Objective Tone' Checklist

  • Use "hedging" language: Foften, tend to, in many cases, it seems that..., it can be argued that...
  • Focus on ideas, not feelings: Instead of "I feel...", write "It is..." or "This suggests...".
  • Use broad examples: "For example, in many countries...", "Studies often show that...", "For many people...".
  • Stay formal: Avoid slang or a casual, spoken style.
